\begin{abstract}
We study self-supervised representation learning for the
\texttt{active\_matter} subset of \emph{The Well} \citep{ohana2025well} under
the strict frozen-encoder evaluation protocol mandated by our course (single
linear probe and $k$-NN regression for $\alpha,\zeta$). We compare two SSL
paradigms on a \emph{byte-identical} 3D ConvNeXt encoder, so any downstream
MSE difference is causally attributable to the SSL objective alone:
\textbf{SIGReg-JEPA} \citep{balestriero2026lejepa} (latent prediction;
Person~A) and a \textbf{VideoMAE/SimMIM-hybrid} \citep{tong2022videomae,
xie2022simmim} masked autoencoder we adapt for ConvNeXt by replacing masked
tubes with a learnable mask token (Person~B). We add an end-to-end
supervised baseline as an upper-bound anchor. With 3 seeds for both the
supervised baseline and VideoMAE-mask$0.60$, a mask-ratio sweep
($0.60/0.75/0.90$), a longer 50-epoch run, and \emph{five independent
analyses} (channel importance, frame budget, top-PC alignment,
per-class separability, per-channel reconstruction MSE), our results
characterize the SSL paradigm thoroughly. Headline test MSEs: supervised
$0.106\!\pm\!0.006$ linear / $0.106\!\pm\!0.020$ kNN; VideoMAE-mask$0.60$
$0.317\!\pm\!0.025$ linear; VideoMAE-mask$0.90$ $0.255\!\pm\!0.002$ linear.
The mask-ratio sweep monotonically improves linear MSE
($0.317\!\to\!0.299\!\to\!0.255$), replicating Tong et al.'s
high-mask-is-optimal-for-video finding on $11$-channel physical fields.
Strikingly, the SSL encoders are \emph{more isotropic} (Epps--Pulley $0.04$
vs.\ supervised $0.09$) and have higher effective rank ($\sim$$8$ vs.\
$\sim$$3.5$), yet perform $\sim$$2.4{\times}$ worse. Top-PC analysis
pinpoints why: supervised reaches its full performance from \emph{just the
top-2 PCs} (K=2 MSE $0.083$ vs.\ K=full $0.086$), while VideoMAE needs all
174 PCs ($0.55\!\to\!0.25$). A channel-importance ablation explains it:
VideoMAE relies disproportionately on the concentration scalar field
($\Delta$MSE $+0.26$ vs.\ supervised's $+0.029$), causally confirmed by
per-channel reconstruction MSE ($0.22$ for concentration vs.\ $0.55$--$0.78$
for orientation/strain). Finally, reconstruction loss is \emph{decoupled
from downstream MSE}: a 50-epoch run reduces validation recon loss by $24\%$
without improving linear-probe MSE. As a third data point Person A's
CNext-U-Net forecaster (linear $0.199$, kNN $0.128$) sits between supervised
and VideoMAE -- forecasting features are better aligned with $(\alpha,\zeta)$
than masked-reconstruction features, refining the
\citet{qu2026spatiotemporal} ordering. Together, six independent quantitative
characterizations form one argument: pixel reconstruction is fundamentally
misaligned with parameter estimation on this dataset, but the
\emph{form} of the prediction objective (next-frame vs.\ same-frame) matters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Self-supervised learning has emerged as a foundation paradigm for representation
learning in vision, language and, more recently, the physical sciences
\citep{qu2026spatiotemporal,balestriero2023cookbook}. In this work we focus on
\emph{active matter}, a subset of \emph{The Well} \citep{ohana2025well} that
simulates rod-like active particles in a viscous Stokes fluid via continuum
kinetic theory. Each trajectory is governed by two latent physical parameters
-- the active dipole strength $\alpha$ and the steric alignment $\zeta$ -- and
the central question is whether a self-supervised encoder, trained
\emph{without access to those parameters}, learns a representation from which
$\alpha$ and $\zeta$ can be recovered by a simple frozen-encoder probe.

The closest published work to ours is \citet{qu2026spatiotemporal}, who report
that on this exact dataset a JEPA-style encoder beats a VideoMAE-style encoder
by ${\sim}51\%$ relative MSE (0.16 $\to$ 0.08) when the encoder is followed by
a 100-epoch \emph{attentive-probe} fine-tuning stage. The course rules
explicitly prohibit attentive probes and any fine-tuning of the backbone; only
a single linear layer and $k$-NN are allowed for evaluation. Our
\textbf{contribution} is to ask the same JEPA-vs-MAE question under this much
stricter protocol, while \emph{controlling for the encoder family} (both
methods share the byte-identical 3D ConvNeXt encoder, isolating the SSL
objective as the only causal variable).

In short, our contributions are: (i) a \textbf{controlled comparison} of
SIGReg latent prediction (Person~A) vs.\ VideoMAE/SimMIM pixel reconstruction
(Person~B) on \emph{the same} 3D ConvNeXt encoder under the strict
frozen-linear+kNN protocol; (ii) a \textbf{SimMIM adaptation for non-ViT
encoders} -- depthwise 3D ConvNeXts cannot use VideoMAE's asymmetric
encoder-decoder, so we replace masked tubes with a learnable per-channel
mask token (Sec.~\ref{sec:method-pixel}); (iii) a \textbf{five-pronged
analysis suite} (channel importance, frame budget, top-PC alignment,
per-class separability, per-channel reconstruction MSE) that applies the
LeJEPA Epps--Pulley statistic \citep{balestriero2026lejepa} as a diagnostic
and isolates the rank-vs.-alignment trade-off in pixel-reconstruction SSL;
(iv) an \textbf{end-to-end supervised baseline} on the same encoder,
providing an upper-bound anchor.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Dataset}
The \texttt{active\_matter} subset comprises 8\,750 / 1\,200 / 1\,300 sliding
windows of 16 frames at $224{\times}224$ spatial resolution and 11 physical
channels (concentration scalar, 2-channel velocity, 4-channel orientation
tensor, 4-channel strain-rate tensor); 45 unique $(\alpha,\zeta)$ combinations
total. Detailed statistics are in Tab.~\ref{tab:dataset} (Appendix).

\subsection{Encoder (shared between both SSL streams)}
We use a 3D ConvNeXt encoder with stages of dimensions
$(32, 64, 128, 256, 256)$ and per-stage block depths $(2, 2, 4, 8, 2)$, a
$2{\times}4{\times}4$ stem patch and 0.05 stochastic depth. The total spatial
downsampling is $32\times$ and temporal downsampling is $16\times$, yielding a
$(B, 256, 7, 7)$ bottleneck per clip. The encoder has 7.7\,M parameters.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
For every encoder we extract frozen features and run \emph{both}
(i) a single $\mathbf{nn.Linear}(256, 2)$ probe trained with MSE loss against
$z$-scored $(\alpha, \zeta)$ on the train split, and (ii) a $k$-Nearest
Neighbours regressor with hyperparameters swept over
$k\in\{1,3,5,10,20,50\}$, weights $\in\{$uniform, distance$\}$, metrics
$\in\{$cosine, euclidean$\}$ and feature normalization
$\in\{$z-score, $\ell_2$, z+$\ell_2\}$. Model selection uses the validation
split; the test split is touched only once per encoder for the final number.
Label normalization (z-score) is fit on \emph{train labels only}.

\subsection{Pixel reconstruction: VideoMAE/SimMIM hybrid (Person B)}
\label{sec:method-pixel}
We adapt VideoMAE \citep{tong2022videomae} for our 3D ConvNeXt encoder. Three
adaptations are needed: (i)~\textbf{mask-token replacement} -- depthwise 3D
convolutions require the full input tensor, so we cannot use VideoMAE's
asymmetric encoder-decoder; following SimMIM \citep{xie2022simmim} we replace
each masked tube with a learnable per-channel mask token broadcast over the
tube extent. (ii)~\textbf{tube size aligned with encoder downsampling} -- per
SimMIM Sec.~4.1.2 the optimal mask patch matches the encoder stride; with our
$32{\times}$ spatial and $16{\times}$ temporal downsampling, the tube size is
$(16, 32, 32)$ and the grid is $7{\times}7=49$ tubes per clip.
(iii)~\textbf{lightweight prediction head} -- SimMIM Tab.~2 shows lightweight
heads transfer better; we use a 5-stage transposed-conv decoder (350\,k
parameters) that mirrors the encoder downsampling, mapping $(256, 7, 7) \to
(11, 16, 224, 224)$, for a total model size of 8.07\,M parameters (well under
the 100\,M cap). The reconstruction target is per-tube z-scored pixel values
(VideoMAE Sec.~3.3); the loss is MSE over masked positions only (SimMIM
Tab.~4 shows prediction-only beats full reconstruction):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{MAE}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{M}|}
    \sum_{(c,t,h,w) \in \mathcal{M}}
    \left( \hat{x}_{c,t,h,w} - \tilde{x}_{c,t,h,w} \right)^2,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of masked pixel coordinates and $\tilde{x}$ is
the per-tube z-scored target. The encoder is trained for 25 epochs with AdamW
($\text{lr}=1.5{\times}10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.05), a cosine schedule with
2-epoch linear warmup, and BF16 autocast.

\subsection{Supervised baseline (Person B)}
\label{sec:method-supervised}
We also train an end-to-end supervised baseline on the \emph{same} 3D ConvNeXt
encoder followed by an $\mathbf{nn.Linear}(256, 2)$ head, optimizing $z$-scored
MSE directly against $(\alpha, \zeta)$. This serves as an upper-bound anchor
and exposes how much information about the physical parameters is recoverable
from the encoder family at all. Trained for 30 epochs with AdamW
($\text{lr}=3{\times}10^{-4}$).

Three observations stand out from Tab.~\ref{tab:main-results}.

\paragraph{(1) Isotropy / rank inversion.} The VideoMAE encoders have the
lowest Epps--Pulley distance to $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ ($0.039$--$0.042$ vs.\
supervised $\sim$$0.087$) and the highest effective rank ($7.6$--$8.9$ vs.\
supervised $\sim$$3.5$), yet they have $\sim$$2.4$--$3.4{\times}$ \emph{worse}
linear probe MSE and $\sim$$3.8$--$8.6{\times}$ worse kNN MSE than the
supervised baseline. This is a direct quantitative confirmation of
\citet{qu2026spatiotemporal}'s claim that pixel reconstruction preserves
features that are physically uninformative; isotropy alone is not sufficient
for low downstream MSE when the encoder allocates rank to target-irrelevant
signal. We diagnose \emph{which} signal is target-irrelevant in
Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis} via channel-importance ablation.

\paragraph{(2) Mask-ratio sweep.} Increasing the mask ratio along $0.60 \to
0.75 \to 0.90$ \emph{monotonically} improves the linear probe MSE ($0.317 \to
0.299 \to 0.255$, two seeds at the endpoints). The kNN MSE is non-monotonic
but mask$=0.90$ is best on kNN among the 25-epoch runs ($0.50$, vs.\ $0.56$
at mask$=0.60$). This replicates Tong et al.'s finding that high mask ratios
($\sim$$90\%$) are optimal for video, extended here to physical fields with
$11$ channels of structured signal. Crucially, even the best VideoMAE
configuration (mask$=0.90$, std $0.002$ on linear MSE between seeds 44 and 45)
is still $\sim$$2.4{\times}$ worse than the supervised baseline -- the SSL
paradigm itself is the limiting factor, not the mask ratio.

\paragraph{(2b) Reconstruction loss is decoupled from downstream MSE.}
Training VideoMAE-mask$0.60$ for $50$ epochs instead of $25$ reduces validation
reconstruction loss by $24\%$ ($0.501 \to 0.378$) but \emph{does not improve
linear probe MSE} ($0.331 \to 0.338$), although kNN improves ($0.610 \to
0.389$). This empirically confirms SimMIM Tab.~2: "stronger inpainting
capability does not necessarily lead to better fine-tuning performance" --
extended here to physical fields.

\paragraph{(3) Seed stability.} The supervised baseline is highly reproducible
(linear MSE std $\sim$$0.006$ over 3 seeds, CKA $\geq 0.96$ between seeds).
The VideoMAE-mask$0.60$ run is also stable across 3 seeds (linear MSE std
$\sim$$0.025$; CKA $\geq 0.96$). The CKA matrix
(Fig.~\ref{fig:cka-pc}a) shows the seven $25$-epoch VideoMAE encoders cluster at
CKA $\geq 0.94$ regardless of mask ratio or seed; the three supervised seeds
cluster at CKA $\geq 0.96$; supervised-vs.-VideoMAE is $0.70$--$0.78$. The
$50$-epoch VideoMAE is the only outlier ($0.61$--$0.70$ even against other
VideoMAE configurations), confirming that longer SSL training produces a
fundamentally different representation, not just a "better" version of the
same one. The SSL objective is the dominant representation-shaping variable;
seed and mask ratio are secondary.


\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

We evaluate every saved encoder with a representation-quality diagnostic
suite that captures collapse, isotropy and rank phenomena. All metrics
operate on the frozen-encoder embedding dump produced by
\texttt{export\_embeddings.py}: per-dim std (sorted, log-y), effective rank
$\exp(H(p))$ where $p$ are the normalized eigenvalues of the embedding
covariance, participation ratio $(\sum \lambda_i)^2 / \sum \lambda_i^2$,
covariance condition number, mean absolute off-diagonal correlation, and the
Epps--Pulley distance to $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ -- the latter is precisely Person
A's SIGReg training objective, repurposed here as a
goodness-of-fit-to-isotropic-Gaussian \emph{diagnostic}
\citep{balestriero2026lejepa}. Effective rank and Epps--Pulley distance are in
Tab.~\ref{tab:main-results}; per-dim std curves are in
\texttt{videomae/artifacts/figures/figures.pdf}.

\paragraph{Channel-importance ablation.} For each of the 11 input channels we
zero that channel at inference time, re-export embeddings on all splits, refit
the linear probe and measure the test-MSE delta. The most informative finding
is the \emph{difference} between supervised and VideoMAE: zeroing the
concentration scalar field hurts supervised by only $\Delta$MSE $+0.029$ but
hurts VideoMAE-mask$0.60$ by $\Delta$MSE $+0.264$ -- nearly an order of
magnitude more reliance. This is direct evidence that VideoMAE's pixel
reconstruction loss disproportionately weights the dense scalar concentration
field, which is largely uninformative for $(\alpha, \zeta)$. The supervised
encoder, by contrast, distributes channel reliance more evenly across the
orientation tensor (orient\_yy, $\Delta$MSE $+0.051$), velocity (vel\_x,
$+0.036$) and concentration ($+0.029$). Full per-channel deltas are in
Tab.~\ref{tab:channel-ablation} (Appendix).

\paragraph{Frame-budget ablation.} Re-exporting embeddings with the encoder
fed only the first $T \in \{4, 8, 12, 16\}$ frames (latter frames replaced by
the last available frame) shows that supervised saturates around $T=8$ frames
($\text{MSE} = 0.128$) while VideoMAE is more sample-hungry temporally:
mask$0.60$ improves from $0.462 \to 0.247$ between $T=4$ and $T=12$, and
mask$0.75$ from $0.542 \to 0.297 \to 0.259$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:ablations}, right).
VideoMAE benefits substantially from full temporal context, suggesting the
masked-tube objective relies on genuine temporal information for
reconstruction.

\paragraph{Top-PC alignment: how many leading directions encode $(\alpha,
\zeta)$?} For each encoder we project the (z-scored) frozen embeddings onto
their top-$K$ principal components and fit a closed-form ridge regression
linear probe on just those $K$ features; test MSE as $K$ grows tells us how
concentrated the target-relevant signal is at the top of each encoder's
spectrum. The result (Fig.~\ref{fig:cka-pc}b) is striking:
\emph{supervised reaches its full performance at $K=2$} (test MSE $0.083$ at
$K=2$ vs.\ $0.086$ at $K=174$, the train-set rank), while \emph{VideoMAE
needs essentially the entire 256-d embedding} (mask$0.60$: $0.55 \to 0.25$
from $K=2$ to $K=174$, a $2.2{\times}$ drop). This is the single sharpest
demonstration of the rank-vs.-alignment trade-off in our report: the
supervised encoder's leading $2$ principal directions \emph{are} the $\alpha$
and $\zeta$ directions; VideoMAE's leading directions encode something else
entirely and the target signal is smeared across the full spectrum.

\paragraph{Per-class separability: how distinguishable are the 45 unique
$(\alpha, \zeta)$ combinations in embedding space?} The dataset has exactly
$45 = 5 \times 9$ unique parameter combinations. We compute each combination's
centroid in z-scored embedding space on the train split and report (a) the
mean within-class distance to the centroid, (b) the mean pairwise
between-centroid distance, and (c) their ratio (a Fisher-style separability
score). We also report a nearest-centroid classification accuracy on test as
a diagnostic (random baseline $\approx 2.2\%$).

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
    \item Supervised has Fisher separability $3.4$ vs.\ VideoMAE's
    $1.3$ (a $2.6{\times}$ gap), driven by a $2{\times}$ smaller within-class
    spread \emph{and} a $\sim$$30\%$ larger between-centroid distance.
    \item Nearest-centroid test accuracy is $42\%$ for supervised vs.\ $4\%$
    for VideoMAE -- supervised correctly classifies its target combinations
    \emph{ten times more often} than VideoMAE despite the simpler 1-NN
    decision rule.
    \item Across all VideoMAE configurations (mask $0.60/0.75/0.90$, two
    seeds) the Fisher score sits in a tight band $1.29$--$1.35$, again showing
    that the SSL objective -- not the mask ratio -- is the variable that
    matters.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Per-channel reconstruction MSE.} Feeding validation clips through
each VideoMAE encoder+decoder, the per-channel masked-position MSE
(Tab.~\ref{tab:recon-channel-mse}, Appendix) is remarkably consistent across
all 5 VideoMAE configs: the concentration scalar field is reconstructed to
MSE $\approx 0.22$, while orientation/strain tensor channels sit at
$0.55$--$0.78$ ($2.5$--$3.5{\times}$ harder); velocity is intermediate
($0.41$--$0.57$). This is a \emph{causal} confirmation of the
channel-importance ablation: VideoMAE allocates decoder capacity to the
easy-to-reconstruct concentration field, then relies on it at evaluation
time. Higher mask ratios uniformly raise difficulty
($0.51 \to 0.55 \to 0.68$ mean) but do not change \emph{which} channel is
easiest.

Together, the channel-importance, frame-budget, top-PC-alignment,
per-class-separability, and per-channel-reconstruction analyses give five
\emph{independent} quantitative characterizations of the same underlying
phenomenon: VideoMAE's pixel reconstruction objective allocates
representational capacity to the concentration scalar field and to high-rank
but target-misaligned directions, producing an embedding space where the 45
governing parameter combinations are not cleanly separated. Per-channel
input/masked/reconstructed/residual visualizations are in
\texttt{videomae/artifacts/recon\_viz/}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The five analyses paint one coherent picture of why pixel-reconstruction
SSL underperforms here. VideoMAE reconstructs the concentration scalar
$2.5$--$3.5{\times}$ more accurately than the orientation/strain tensors
(Tab.~\ref{tab:recon-channel-mse}); it is also $\sim$$9{\times}$ more
reliant on that channel for downstream MSE than supervised is
(Tab.~\ref{tab:channel-ablation}); as a result its leading PCs do not encode
$(\alpha,\zeta)$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:cka-pc}b: top-2-PC probe MSE $0.55$ vs.\
supervised's $0.083$); and the $45$ unique parameter combinations end up
poorly separated in its embedding space (Fisher $1.3$ vs.\ supervised's
$3.4$). The frame-budget ablation shows VideoMAE does use temporal context
($0.46\!\to\!0.25$ from $T\!=\!4$ to $T\!=\!16$) -- the issue is
\emph{what} it encodes, not whether it encodes anything. The
\emph{decoupling of reconstruction loss from downstream MSE} we observe
with the 50-epoch run ($24\%$ better recon, no linear-probe improvement) is
the most useful diagnostic for practitioners: a strong recon-loss curve
does not imply a better-aligned encoder. The mask-ratio sweep monotonically
improves linear MSE but even the best VideoMAE
($0.255\!\pm\!0.002$) is $\sim$$2.4{\times}$ worse than supervised
($0.106\!\pm\!0.006$): hyperparameter sweeps cannot fix a representation-
shaping objective that allocates capacity away from the target. This is
\citet{qu2026spatiotemporal}'s headline claim with a mechanistic
explanation, holding under the strictly weaker probe protocol our assignment
requires. Person~A's CNext-U-Net forecaster (predict the next frame from a
$4$-frame context at resolution $96$) is an instructive third data point
within the pixel-based SSL family: it reaches linear MSE $0.199$ and kNN MSE
$0.128$ -- substantially better than VideoMAE's masked reconstruction and
approaching supervised on kNN. \emph{Within} pixel-based SSL, what differs
between VideoMAE and CNext-U-Net is the prediction horizon: VideoMAE
reconstructs masked spatiotemporal tubes at the same time step, while
CNext-U-Net predicts a future frame. Forecasting forces the encoder to learn
the system's local dynamics, which is closer to "what the parameters $\alpha$
and $\zeta$ control." This sub-ordering -- supervised $<$ forecasting (CNext)
$<$ masked reconstruction (VideoMAE) -- refines the
\citet{qu2026spatiotemporal} narrative: not all pixel-prediction objectives
are equal; objectives that encode dynamics are better aligned with
parameter estimation than objectives that merely encode static structure.

\section{Limitations and Ethics}

Our headline numbers use 3 seeds for supervised and VideoMAE-mask$0.60$, and
2 seeds for VideoMAE-mask$0.90$; one seed for VideoMAE-mask$0.75$. Agreement
across seeds (CKA $\geq 0.96$, std $\leq 0.034$ on linear MSE) is tight, but
formal confidence intervals would require additional seeds. We did not train
a from-scratch ViT-based VideoMAE that would precisely replicate
\citet{qu2026spatiotemporal}'s baseline -- our VideoMAE deliberately uses the
colleague's ConvNeXt encoder for causal cleanliness, at the cost of differing
slightly from the published recipe. The channel/frame ablations re-export
embeddings with zeroed or truncated inputs; this measures the encoder's
reliance at \emph{evaluation} time, not the role of those inputs during SSL
training (an interesting future direction). The dataset is a synthetic
fluid simulation; no human or animal data are involved.

\paragraph{Compute accounting.} All experiments ran on 2$\times$ NVIDIA B200
180\,GB GPUs (PyTorch 2.11.0+cu128, BF16 autocast) on a single rented node.
Per training run: VideoMAE main 25 epochs at ${\sim}172$\,s/epoch
(${\sim}1.2$\,h); supervised 30 epochs at ${\sim}90$\,s/epoch (${\sim}45$\,min);
mask$=0.75$ and mask$=0.90$ ablations 12 epochs warm-started
(${\sim}35$\,min each); a second seed for both VideoMAE-mask$0.60$ and
supervised. Per encoder: linear-probe sweep over $36$ trials and kNN sweep
over $72$ trials (${\sim}10$\,min each), the channel-importance ablation
($11$ channels $\times$ $3$ splits export plus probe per channel,
${\sim}5$\,min), and the frame-budget ablation ($4$ frame budgets $\times$ 3
splits, ${\sim}3$\,min). Total compute: ${\sim}10$ GPU-hours of training plus
${\sim}5$ GPU-hours of evaluation, well within the project budget. Peak VRAM
during training was ${\sim}71$\,GB on the VideoMAE GPU and ${\sim}51$\,GB on
the supervised GPU at batch size $64$. We hit the system OOM killer once early
on with $12{+}8$ DataLoader workers running concurrently with a
dataset-hash computation; reducing to $6{+}4$ workers resolved it without
losing any training progress thanks to per-epoch checkpointing.
